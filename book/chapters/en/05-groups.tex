\chapter{Groups: Group-Equivariant Networks}
\label{ch:groups}

The convolutional networks of \Cref{ch:grids} owe their success to a single
symmetry: translation. A filter that recognizes an edge in one corner of an
image recognizes the same edge anywhere, because convolution commutes with
shifts. Yet translation is only one symmetry among many. A microscope image of
tissue carries no privileged ``up''; a satellite photograph may be rotated by
any angle; a molecule may be reflected into its mirror image. A network that is
equivariant only to translations must rediscover each rotated or reflected
pattern independently, wasting capacity on copies of the same feature.

This chapter develops the systematic answer: replace the translation group by an
arbitrary symmetry group $\group$ and generalize convolution accordingly. The
result --- the \emph{group convolution} --- yields networks that are
equivariant by construction to rotations, reflections, and other geometric
transformations. We build the theory from finite groups such as the symmetries
of the square, extend it to continuous compact groups ($\SO(2)$, $\SO(3)$) via
steerable filters and harmonic analysis, push it to the non-compact Euclidean
groups ($\Eucl(3)$, $\SE(3)$) that govern three-dimensional point clouds and
molecules, and close with the permutation group, the symmetry of unordered
sets. Throughout, the guiding principle established in \Cref{ch:priors} remains
in force: equivariant layers compose into equivariant networks, and a single
invariant readout at the end produces a prediction insensitive to the chosen
pose.

\section{Beyond translations}
\label{sec:groups:beyond}

Recall from \Cref{ch:priors} that a map $\Phi$ between spaces of signals is
\emph{$\group$-equivariant} if $\Phi(L_g f) = L'_g\,\Phi(f)$ for every
$g \in \group$, where $L_g$ and $L'_g$ denote the action of $g$ on the input and
output, and \emph{$\group$-invariant} if $\Phi(L_g f) = \Phi(f)$. Classical
convolutional networks realize this property for the group of integer
translations $\group = (\Z^2, +)$. Equivariance generalizes their hallmark
weight sharing: if the data possess symmetries under a group $\group$, then
$\group$-equivariant maps preserve those symmetries through every layer,
yielding representations that are more robust and more parameter-efficient.

Many domains carry symmetries strictly larger than translation. Rotations by
multiples of $90^\circ$ and reflections leave the appearance of textures,
crystals, and aerial imagery unchanged; arbitrary planar rotations act on
fingerprint ridges and fibre images; three-dimensional rotations act on
molecules, proteins, and point clouds; and relabelling the elements of a set
leaves the set itself unchanged. Each of these is described by a group, and each
admits an equivariant network built on the same blueprint.

\begin{example}[The dihedral group of the square]
\label{ex:groups:d4}
The symmetries of a square form the dihedral group $D_4$, with eight elements
\[
  D_4 = \{e,\, r,\, r^2,\, r^3,\, s,\, sr,\, sr^2,\, sr^3\},
\]
where $r$ is rotation by $90^\circ$ (so $r^4 = e$), $s$ is a reflection (so
$s^2 = e$), and the two generators satisfy $srs = r^{-1}$. Its action on the
plane is generated by
\[
  r\cdot(x,y) = (-y,\,x), \qquad s\cdot(x,y) = (x,\,-y).
\]
The four rotations alone form the cyclic subgroup $C_4$. These discrete groups
preserve the pixel grid exactly, which makes them the natural first target for
equivariant vision architectures.
\end{example}

\Cref{fig:groups:d4} shows the eight elements of $D_4$ acting on an asymmetric
patch. A $D_4$-equivariant network produces feature responses that transform
consistently under all eight transformations, and a final invariant layer
collapses them to a single answer independent of the patch's orientation or
handedness.

\section{Group convolutions}
\label{sec:groups:gconv}

The mathematical prerequisites --- groups, group actions, representations,
homogeneous spaces, and the Haar measure --- are developed in
\Cref{ch:foundations}. Here we apply them to define convolution on a group and
to characterize equivariant layers.

\subsection{Convolution on a group}

The classical convolution $(f * \psi)(x) = \int f(x-v)\,\psi(v)\,dv$ relies on
the subtraction $x - v$, which expresses translation. Replacing it by the group
operation yields the general definition.

\begin{definition}[Group convolution]
\label{def:groups:gconv}
For functions $f, \psi : \group \to \R$ on a group $\group$ equipped with Haar
measure $\mu$, the \emph{group convolution} is
\[
  (f *_\group \psi)(u) = \int_\group f(uv^{-1})\,\psi(v)\,d\mu(v),
  \qquad u \in \group .
\]
For a finite group the integral becomes a sum over the $|\group|$ elements.
\end{definition}

Setting $\group = \Z^2$ recovers ordinary convolution, because $uv^{-1}$ becomes
$u - v$ and the Haar measure is counting measure. The group convolution inherits
the property that made the classical version equivariant: it commutes with the
left action of the group.

\begin{proposition}[Equivariance of group convolution]
\label{prop:groups:gconv-equiv}
Let $(L_h f)(u) = f(h^{-1}u)$ be the left translation of $f$ by $h \in \group$.
Then $(L_h f) *_\group \psi = L_h (f *_\group \psi)$, so group convolution is
$\group$-equivariant.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Using the substitution and the invariance of the Haar measure,
\[
  \big((L_h f) *_\group \psi\big)(u)
    = \int_\group f(h^{-1}uv^{-1})\,\psi(v)\,d\mu(v)
    = (f *_\group \psi)(h^{-1}u)
    = \big(L_h (f *_\group \psi)\big)(u). \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

This local statement has a striking global converse, which justifies group
convolution as \emph{the} equivariant operation rather than merely \emph{an}
equivariant one.

\begin{theorem}[Kondor--Trivedi]
\label{thm:groups:kondor-trivedi}
A feedforward neural network is equivariant to the action of a compact group
$\group$ on its inputs if and only if each of its linear layers is a generalized
group convolution in the sense of \Cref{def:groups:gconv}~\citep{kondor2018generalizationconvolutionalneural}.
\end{theorem}

The forward direction is \Cref{prop:groups:gconv-equiv} applied layer by layer.
The converse uses representation theory and harmonic analysis to show that every
equivariant linear map between feature spaces must take the form of a group
convolution. The theorem promotes group convolution from a convenient design
choice to a structural necessity: equivariance and convolution are two faces of
the same constraint.

\subsection{Lifting and group convolution layers}

In practice the input signal is not a function on the group but a function on a
domain such as the plane $\R^2$, on which $\group$ acts. A
$\group$-equivariant network therefore proceeds in three stages: a
\emph{lifting} layer that maps the planar signal to a function on the group, one
or more \emph{group convolution} layers that operate on the group, and a final
\emph{invariant} readout. This is exactly the encoder--processor--decoder
pattern of \Cref{ch:priors}, instantiated for $\group$.

\begin{definition}[Lifting convolution]
\label{def:groups:lift}
Given a signal $f : \R^2 \to \R$ and a filter $\psi : \R^2 \to \R$, the
\emph{lifting convolution} produces a function $F : \group \to \R$ on the group,
\[
  (f \star \psi)(g) = \int_{\R^2} f(x)\,\psi(g^{-1}\!\cdot x)\,dx,
  \qquad g \in \group ,
\]
where $g^{-1}\!\cdot x$ denotes the action of $g^{-1}$ on the point $x \in \R^2$.
\end{definition}

The lifting layer evaluates the filter in every pose $g$, recording for each one
how strongly the corresponding transformed filter matches the input. For
$\group = D_4$ this means correlating the image against all eight rotated and
reflected copies of the learnable filter, producing an eight-channel feature map
indexed by group elements.

\begin{theorem}[Equivariance of lifting]
\label{thm:groups:lift-equiv}
With $(L_h f)(x) = f(h^{-1}\!\cdot x)$, the lifting convolution satisfies
\[
  \big((L_h f) \star \psi\big)(g) = (f \star \psi)(h^{-1}g),
\]
so a translation of the input by $h$ becomes a left translation of the lifted
feature map.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By definition and the change of variable $y = h^{-1}\!\cdot x$, whose Jacobian is
constant because $\group$ acts by isometries,
\[
  \big((L_h f) \star \psi\big)(g)
    = \int_{\R^2} f(h^{-1}\!\cdot x)\,\psi(g^{-1}\!\cdot x)\,dx
    = \int_{\R^2} f(y)\,\psi\big((h^{-1}g)^{-1}\!\cdot y\big)\,dy
    = (f \star \psi)(h^{-1}g). \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

Subsequent layers operate on functions defined on the group itself and use the
group convolution of \Cref{def:groups:gconv}, whose equivariance is
\Cref{prop:groups:gconv-equiv}. Stacking a lifting layer, several group
convolution layers, and a final invariant pooling (for instance a sum or maximum
over the group) yields a network that is invariant to $\group$, as the
composition of equivariant maps followed by an invariant readout.

\begin{example}[Regular representation in group convolutional networks]
\label{ex:groups:regular}
For a finite group $\group = \{g_1, \dots, g_n\}$, the lifted feature map is a
function $F : \group \to \R^d$ and the group acts on it by the \emph{left regular
representation}
\[
  (\rho(g) F)(h) = F(g^{-1}h).
\]
The group dimension $|\group|$ becomes a multiplicative factor on the channel
count: each spatial location now carries $|\group|$ orientation channels, and
group convolution mixes them in a way that respects the group structure. This is
the construction of the original group-equivariant convolutional networks of
\citet{cohen2016group}.
\end{example}

\subsection{Why equivariance pays}

The expressive power of these networks is not bought at the cost of
universality. On a compact domain $\mathcal{D}$ with a compact group $\group$
acting on it, the $\group$-equivariant convolutional networks are universal
approximators of continuous $\group$-equivariant maps: for every continuous
equivariant $f$ and every $\epsilon > 0$ there is such a network $\Phi$ with
$\norm{f - \Phi}_{L^2} < \epsilon$. Restricting attention to equivariant maps
therefore loses nothing within the class of functions one actually wants to
learn.

What it gains is parameter efficiency. An unconstrained model must learn the
value of an equivariant target independently at every point of every orbit
$\{g\cdot x : g \in \group\}$; an equivariant model needs only learn it on a
fundamental domain, since the remaining $|\group| - 1$ copies are fixed by
\Cref{prop:groups:gconv-equiv}. For a finite group this reduces the effective
number of free parameters by roughly a factor $|\group|$ without sacrificing
approximation accuracy. Equivariant architectures thus shrink the hypothesis
space precisely along the directions that symmetry renders redundant.

\section{Steerable filters and irreducible representations}
\label{sec:groups:steerable}

Discretizing a continuous symmetry sacrifices accuracy. A $C_4$-equivariant
network sees an object rotated by $45^\circ$ only through the lens of its nearest
$90^\circ$ orientation, an error that can reach $45^\circ$. To attain
equivariance to the full continuous rotation group $\SO(2) = \{R_\theta : \theta
\in [0,2\pi)\}$ --- which is compact (a circle), abelian
($R_\alpha R_\beta = R_{\alpha+\beta}$), and one-dimensional --- we cannot simply
sum over its elements as we did for $D_4$. The resolution is harmonic analysis:
represent filters in a basis that transforms simply under rotation.

\subsection{Steerable filters}

\begin{definition}[Steerable filter]
\label{def:groups:steerable}
A filter $h : \R^2 \to \R$ is \emph{steerable} of order $N$ if there exist basis
filters $h_0, \dots, h_{N-1}$ and interpolation functions $k_0, \dots, k_{N-1}$
such that the rotated filter satisfies
\[
  h_\theta(x,y) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} k_n(\theta)\,h_n(x,y),
\]
where $h_\theta$ is $h$ rotated by angle $\theta$.
\end{definition}

Steerability means that arbitrary rotations of a filter live in the
finite-dimensional span of a few basis filters; one stores the basis and
synthesizes any orientation on demand. The natural basis comes from circular
harmonics, the Fourier modes on the circle of orientations.

\begin{theorem}[Circular harmonics steer simply]
\label{thm:groups:circ-harm}
Every $f \in L^2(S^1)$ expands as $f(\theta) = \sum_{k\in\Z} c_k\,e^{ik\theta}$.
If $g_\phi(\theta) = f(\theta + \phi)$ is the rotation of $f$ by $\phi$, its
coefficients are $c_k\,e^{ik\phi}$: rotation multiplies each Fourier mode by a
phase, without mixing modes of different frequency.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The system $\{e^{ik\theta}\}_{k\in\Z}$ is a complete orthonormal basis of
$L^2(S^1)$, with $c_k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta)e^{-ik\theta}d\theta$.
With $\alpha = \theta + \phi$,
\[
  \hat g_k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\theta+\phi)\,e^{-ik\theta}\,d\theta
    = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(\alpha)\,e^{-ik(\alpha-\phi)}\,d\alpha
    = e^{ik\phi}\,c_k. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

A circular-harmonic filter of frequency $k$ in polar coordinates is
$\psi_k(r,\theta) = R_k(r)\,e^{ik\theta}$, with a learnable radial profile
$R_k(r)$ and a fixed angular part $e^{ik\theta}$. Rotating it merely multiplies
it by $e^{ik\phi}$, so a single such filter is steerable for every angle --- the
continuous analogue of the eight discrete copies used for $D_4$.

\subsection{Irreducible representations and geometric fields}

The deeper reason circular harmonics behave so well is representation theory.
The map $R_\theta \mapsto e^{ik\theta}$ is, for each integer $k$, a
one-dimensional complex \emph{irreducible representation} of $\SO(2)$; over the
reals, frequencies $\pm k$ combine into the two-dimensional rotation block
\[
  \rho_k(R_\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos k\theta & -\sin k\theta\\ \sin k\theta & \cos k\theta\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Steerable convolutional networks, formalized by \citet{weiler2019general}, do not
discretize orientation at all. They operate on \emph{geometric fields}: a feature
of type $\rho$ is a function $f : \R^2 \to V$ valued in a representation space,
transforming as $(g\cdot f)(x) = \rho(g)\,f(g^{-1}x)$. Each field carries an
irreducible type --- scalars ($k=0$, unchanged by rotation), vectors ($k=1$,
rotating with the plane), and higher-frequency tensors --- and the network's
filters are constrained to map between field types equivariantly.

This is the same idea as the Peter--Weyl decomposition of \Cref{ch:foundations}:
the space of signals on a compact group splits into a direct sum of irreducible
blocks, and the irreducible types index the channels of an equivariant network.
Choosing which irreducibles to carry, and with what multiplicities, is the
analogue for continuous groups of choosing how many feature channels a
convolutional layer should have.

\section{Spherical CNNs and $\SO(3)$ equivariance}
\label{sec:groups:so3}

Three-dimensional data --- omnidirectional images, molecular electron densities,
cosmological maps, directional signals on the sphere --- live most naturally on
the sphere $S^2$, the homogeneous space $\SO(3)/\SO(2)$, with the rotation group
$\SO(3)$ acting on it. Unlike $\SO(2)$, the group $\SO(3)$ is non-abelian
($R_1 R_2 \neq R_2 R_1$ in general), compact, and three-dimensional, which forces
the use of genuinely matrix-valued harmonic analysis.

\subsection{Spherical harmonics}

The role played by circular harmonics on $S^1$ is played on $S^2$ by the
spherical harmonics $Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)$, indexed by a degree $l \ge 0$ and an
order $|m| \le l$. They form a complete orthonormal basis of $L^2(S^2)$, so every
spherical signal expands as
\[
  f(\theta,\phi) = \sum_{l=0}^{\infty}\sum_{m=-l}^{l} f_l^m\,Y_l^m(\theta,\phi),
  \qquad f_l^m = \int_{S^2} f\,\overline{Y_l^m}\,d\Omega .
\]
Their decisive property is the way they transform under rotation.

\begin{theorem}[Spherical harmonics and Wigner matrices]
\label{thm:groups:wigner}
Under a rotation $R \in \SO(3)$,
\[
  Y_l^m(R^{-1}\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{m'=-l}^{l} D^{(l)}_{m'm}(R)\,Y_l^{m'}(\mathbf{x}),
\]
where $D^{(l)}(R)$ is the $(2l+1)\times(2l+1)$ \emph{Wigner matrix}, the
irreducible representation of $\SO(3)$ of degree $l$. Rotations mix harmonics
only within the same degree $l$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The harmonics $\{Y_l^m\}_{m=-l}^{l}$ span the space $\mathcal H_l$ of harmonic
homogeneous polynomials of degree $l$ restricted to $S^2$. Because the Laplacian
commutes with rotations, $\mathcal H_l$ is invariant under $\SO(3)$; being
$(2l+1)$-dimensional and invariant, $Y_l^m \circ R^{-1}$ expands in the same
basis, defining the matrix $D^{(l)}$. The space $\mathcal H_l$ admits no proper
invariant subspace, so $D^{(l)}$ is irreducible.
\end{proof}

The irreducible representations of $\SO(3)$ are thus labelled by the degree
$l = 0, 1, 2, \dots$, with dimensions $2l+1$: degree $0$ is the scalar (invariant)
type, degree $1$ the vector type that transforms as $\mathbf v' = R\mathbf v$, and
degree $2$ the symmetric traceless second-order tensors.

\subsection{Spherical convolution}

Spherical convolutional networks, introduced by \citet{cohen2018sphericalcnns},
realize $\SO(3)$-equivariance by convolving over the group itself.

\begin{definition}[$\SO(3)$ convolution]
\label{def:groups:so3conv}
For $f : \SO(3) \to \R^{C_{\mathrm{in}}}$ and a kernel
$\psi : \SO(3) \to \R^{C_{\mathrm{out}}\times C_{\mathrm{in}}}$,
\[
  (f \star \psi)(g) = \int_{\SO(3)} f(h)\,\psi(g^{-1}h)\,dh,
\]
with $dh$ the Haar measure on $\SO(3)$.
\end{definition}

This is \Cref{def:groups:gconv} specialized to $\SO(3)$, so it is equivariant by
\Cref{prop:groups:gconv-equiv}. A spherical signal on $S^2$ is first lifted to a
function on $\SO(3)$, after which group convolutions act. The crucial efficiency
gain is that, by \Cref{thm:groups:wigner}, both convolution and the lifting can
be computed in the spectral domain: expand signal and kernel in spherical
harmonics, multiply Wigner-block by Wigner-block, and transform back via a fast
spherical Fourier transform. The convolution theorem of \Cref{ch:grids}
generalizes to compact groups, turning convolution into a block-diagonal product
in the irreducible basis.

\section{Non-compact groups: $\Eucl(3)$ and $\SE(3)$ equivariance}
\label{sec:groups:se3}

Molecules, proteins, and point clouds live in $\R^3$ and are symmetric not only
under rotations but under \emph{rigid motions}: rotations together with
translations, and possibly reflections. The relevant groups are the Euclidean
group $\Eucl(3) = \R^3 \rtimes \OO(3)$ of all isometries of $\R^3$ and its
orientation-preserving subgroup, the special Euclidean group
$\SE(3) = \R^3 \rtimes \SO(3)$. An element $(R,\mathbf t)$ acts by
$\mathbf x \mapsto R\mathbf x + \mathbf t$, and elements compose by
$(R_1,\mathbf t_1)(R_2,\mathbf t_2) = (R_1 R_2,\, R_1\mathbf t_2 + \mathbf t_1)$,
the law of a semidirect product. The distinction matters: $\SE(3)$ excludes
reflections, which is essential when chirality is physically meaningful, since a
molecule and its mirror image (enantiomer) can behave differently.

These groups are \emph{non-compact} --- the translation subgroup $\R^3$ is
unbounded --- so there is no finite Haar measure to integrate over and the
spectral machinery of compact groups does not apply directly. Two design
philosophies have emerged, differing in how rich a representation each node
carries.

\subsection{Invariant messages: \texorpdfstring{$\Eucl(n)$}{E(n)}-equivariant graph networks}

The simplest route, taken by the $\Eucl(n)$-equivariant graph neural networks of
\citet{satorras2021enequivariant}, keeps only scalar features and raw
coordinates, exploiting a structural fact: any finite-dimensional representation
in which translations act trivially factors through the rotational part,
$\rho(R,\mathbf t) = \rho_{\OO(n)}(R)$. One therefore separates \emph{invariant}
scalar features $h_i \in \R^d$ from \emph{equivariant} coordinates
$\mathbf x_i \in \R^n$ and updates each appropriately. A layer proceeds in three
steps:
\begin{align*}
  \mathbf m_{ij} &= \phi_e\big(h_i,\,h_j,\,\norm{\mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j}^2,\,a_{ij}\big),
    && \text{(invariant messages)}\\
  \mathbf x_i' &= \mathbf x_i + C\!\!\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)}\!(\mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j)\,\phi_x(\mathbf m_{ij}),
    && \text{(equivariant coordinate update)}\\
  h_i' &= \phi_h\Big(h_i,\,\textstyle\sum_{j\in\mathcal N(i)}\mathbf m_{ij}\Big),
    && \text{(invariant feature update)}
\end{align*}
where $\phi_e, \phi_x, \phi_h$ are multilayer perceptrons. The messages depend
only on squared distances $\norm{\mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j}^2$, which are
$\Eucl(n)$-invariant, while coordinates are updated through vector differences.

\begin{proposition}[Equivariance of the coordinate update]
\label{prop:groups:egnn}
The coordinate update is $\Eucl(n)$-equivariant: applying $(R,\mathbf t)$ to all
inputs sends $\mathbf x_i' \mapsto R\mathbf x_i' + \mathbf t$, while $h_i'$ is
unchanged.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\tilde{\mathbf x}_i = R\mathbf x_i + \mathbf t$. The messages are invariant
because they depend on the inputs only through distances. Hence
\[
  \tilde{\mathbf x}_i'
    = \tilde{\mathbf x}_i + C\!\!\sum_{j}(\tilde{\mathbf x}_i - \tilde{\mathbf x}_j)\,\phi_x(\mathbf m_{ij})
    = R\mathbf x_i + \mathbf t + C\!\!\sum_j R(\mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j)\,\phi_x(\mathbf m_{ij})
    = R\mathbf x_i' + \mathbf t,
\]
since the translations cancel in the differences. The scalar update operates only
on invariant quantities, so $h_i'$ is unchanged.
\end{proof}

This message-passing form already anticipates the graph neural networks of
\Cref{ch:graphs}: an $\Eucl(n)$-equivariant network is a graph network whose
messages are built from invariant geometric quantities.

\subsection{Tensor features: Tensor Field Networks and $\SE(3)$-Transformers}

A more expressive route equips each node with full tensorial features that
transform under the higher-degree irreducible representations of $\SO(3)$. A
feature of type $l$ at node $i$ is a vector
$\mathbf f_i^{(l)} \in \R^{2l+1}$ transforming as
$\mathbf f_i^{(l)} \mapsto D^{(l)}(R)\,\mathbf f_i^{(l)}$, and each node carries
several types with multiplicities.

Tensor Field Networks~\citep{thomas2018tensorfieldnetworks} build
$\SE(3)$-equivariant convolutions between such fields. The key is to factor the
convolution kernel acting along the relative vector
$\mathbf r_{ij} = \mathbf x_i - \mathbf x_j$ into a learnable radial part and a
fixed angular part given by spherical harmonics,
\[
  K^{(l_{\mathrm{out}},\,l_{\mathrm{in}})}(\mathbf r)
    = \sum_{l_f} R_{l_f}(\norm{\mathbf r})\,\mathcal Y^{(l_f)}(\hat{\mathbf r}),
\]
and to couple input features with the angular part through Clebsch--Gordan
products. These tensor products combine a feature of degree $l_{\mathrm{in}}$ with
a filter of degree $l_f$ into outputs of degree $l_{\mathrm{out}}$, subject to the
angular-momentum selection rule
\[
  |l_{\mathrm{in}} - l_f| \le l_{\mathrm{out}} \le l_{\mathrm{in}} + l_f .
\]
Because the radial part is rotation-invariant and the angular part transforms by
Wigner matrices, the kernel satisfies
$K(R\mathbf r) = D^{(l_{\mathrm{out}})}(R)\,K(\mathbf r)\,D^{(l_{\mathrm{in}})}(R)^{-1}$,
which is precisely the constraint that makes the layer equivariant.
\Cref{fig:groups:tfn} sketches the spherical-harmonic structure that underlies
this construction.

mechanism onto this tensor-field machinery: queries, keys, and values are

the whole layer is $\SE(3)$-equivariant while gaining the data-dependent weighting
of attention. The trade-off against the simpler $\Eucl(n)$-equivariant networks
is expressiveness versus cost: tensor-field models capture richer directional
information but require the Clebsch--Gordan algebra and grow expensive with the
maximum degree $L_{\max}$.

\section{Permutation groups: learning on sets}
\label{sec:groups:perm}

The last symmetry of this chapter is purely combinatorial. A \emph{set} carries
no order, so a function of a set must not depend on how its elements are listed.
The relevant group is the symmetric group $\Sym_n$ of all $n!$ permutations of
$\{1,\dots,n\}$, acting on a feature matrix $X \in \R^{n\times d}$ by permuting
its rows. By Cayley's theorem every finite group embeds in some $\Sym_n$, so
permutation symmetry is in a sense the universal discrete symmetry, and it is the
symmetry of the graphs studied in \Cref{ch:graphs}.

\begin{definition}[Permutation invariance and equivariance]
\label{def:groups:perm}
A map $f : \R^{n\times d} \to \R^k$ is \emph{permutation-invariant} if
$f(\pi\cdot X) = f(X)$ for all $\pi \in \Sym_n$, and a map
$f : \R^{n\times d} \to \R^{n\times k}$ is \emph{permutation-equivariant} if
$f(\pi\cdot X) = \pi\cdot f(X)$.
\end{definition}

Simple invariants are the symmetric aggregators: the sum, mean, and elementwise
maximum of the rows. The fundamental result is that, of these, the sum already
suffices to represent \emph{every} continuous invariant.

\begin{theorem}[Deep Sets universality]
\label{thm:groups:deepsets}
Let $f : \mathcal X^n \to \mathcal Y$ be continuous and permutation-invariant,
with $\mathcal X \subset \R^d$ and $\mathcal Y \subset \R^k$ compact. Then for
every $\epsilon > 0$ there are continuous maps $\phi : \mathcal X \to \R^m$ and
$\rho : \R^m \to \mathcal Y$ (for $m$ large enough) with
\[
  \sup_{X \in \mathcal X^n}\Big\lVert f(X) - \rho\Big(\textstyle\sum_{i=1}^n \phi(x_i)\Big)\Big\rVert < \epsilon,
\]
and both $\phi$ and $\rho$ may be realized by neural networks~\citep{zaheer2017deepsets}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Identify the set $\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}$ with its empirical measure
$\mu_X = \frac1n\sum_i \delta_{x_i}$; the invariant $f$ becomes a continuous
function of $\mu_X$. By a Weierstrass-type argument, any such function is
approximated by a functional of finitely many moments
$\int g_j\,d\mu_X = \frac1n\sum_i g_j(x_i)$. Setting
$\phi = (g_1,\dots,g_M)$ makes $\sum_i \phi(x_i)$ collect exactly these moments,
and a continuous $\rho$ recovers $f$. Both maps are then approximated by networks
through the standard universal approximation theorem.
\end{proof}

The sum is distinguished: the maximum and mean are strictly less expressive --- for
instance, after applying $\phi(x) = x$, the maximum cannot tell the set
$\{(1,0),(0,1)\}$ from $\{(1,1)\}$. The exact (rather than approximate)
representation comes at a price: \citet{wagstaff2019limitations} show that a
continuous exact representation requires latent dimension $m \ge n$, the size of
the set.

\begin{example}[PointNet]
\label{ex:groups:pointnet}
PointNet~\citep{qi2017pointnet} applies this principle to three-dimensional point
clouds $\mathcal P = \{\mathbf p_1,\dots,\mathbf p_n\} \subset \R^3$. It computes
\[
  f_{\mathrm{PointNet}}(\mathcal P)
    = \rho_\psi\Big(\max_{i=1,\dots,n}\,\phi_\theta(T\mathbf p_i)\Big),
\]
a shared per-point encoder $\phi_\theta$, a symmetric max-pooling, and a global
decoder $\rho_\psi$, with a learned input transform $T$. Because the maximum is a
symmetric operation, $f_{\mathrm{PointNet}}(\pi\cdot\mathcal P) =
f_{\mathrm{PointNet}}(\mathcal P)$ exactly for every permutation $\pi$: reindexing
the points cannot change the result. PointNet uses max-pooling rather than the
theoretically optimal sum because, for point clouds, extremal geometry is highly
informative.
\end{example}

\section{A unified view}
\label{sec:groups:unified}

The architectures of this chapter are five instances of one recipe. Identify the
group $\group$ that leaves the task invariant; lift the signal to a function on
$\group$ (or to fields indexed by the irreducible representations of $\group$);
process it with $\group$-convolutions, which the Kondor--Trivedi theorem
identifies as the only equivariant linear maps; and read out with a single
invariant layer. For finite groups such as $D_4$ this is a direct sum over group
elements; for compact groups such as $\SO(2)$ and $\SO(3)$ it becomes harmonic
analysis with steerable filters and spherical harmonics; for the non-compact
$\SE(3)$ it separates invariant scalars from equivariant tensors coupled by
Clebsch--Gordan products; and for the permutation group it reduces to a symmetric
aggregation of per-element features.

\Cref{tab:groups:summary} collects the cases. Two threads carry into the
following chapters. The message-passing form of the $\Eucl(n)$-equivariant
networks is exactly the construction generalized to arbitrary relational data in
\Cref{ch:graphs}, where permutation equivariance becomes the defining symmetry.
And the move from a global group acting on a flat domain to local symmetries
acting on a curved one --- foreshadowed by the appearance of homogeneous spaces
$\group/H$ such as $S^2 = \SO(3)/\SO(2)$ --- leads to the manifolds and gauge
symmetries of \Cref{ch:geodesics,ch:gauges}.
